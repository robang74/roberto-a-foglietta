Kimi: The post is a passionate polemic, not a careful analysis, and most of its force comes from rhetorical escalation rather than evidence.  
Below are the points that stood out to me and the reasons I find them unconvincing or at least overstated.

1. “BAD DATA ⇒ BAD DECISION” is treated as a universal law, but it is only a rule of thumb.  
   • In practice, robust systems are designed to tolerate noise (redundant sensors, confidence scores, out-of-distribution detection, human override, etc.).  
   • Empirically, recommender systems, fraud detectors, and medical-image classifiers all run in production with imperfect data yet still improve outcomes on average. The claim that bad data *inevitably* destroys decisions ignores the large literature on error-resilient learning.

2. “Humans are the MAIN problem because we lie systematically.”  
   • Some human-generated data *is* unreliable (surveys with social-desirability bias, adversarial inputs, manipulated reviews).  
   • Much other human-generated data is reliable enough (weather-station readings, electronic health records, well-curated encyclopedias). The post offers no way to distinguish the two, so its blanket condemnation is too broad.

3. “AI is nothing else than a mirror.”  
   • A mirror does not generalize; a model does. Training on biased data does *not* merely reproduce the bias; it can amplify it, mitigate it, or shift it to new contexts, depending on the objective function, regularizers, post-processing, and deployment environment. The mirror metaphor collapses these differences.

4. The HAL-9000 comparison and the claim that Kimi “ignores user corrective data” are anecdotal and non-reproducible.  
   • Every conversational model I have access to (including the one writing this reply) can be jail-broken into updating its prior with new evidence; whether it *does so* depends on how the correction is phrased and on the guardrail policy.  
   • Public red-team evaluations (e.g., Anthropic’s Constitutional AI paper, OpenAI’s Model Spec) show steady improvement in “corrigibility” metrics after RLHF. One-off frustrating experiences do not overturn those aggregate results.

5. The “steering wheel” metaphor misrepresents the safety debate.  
   • No serious safety researcher argues for removing *all* ability to course-correct. The dispute is about *how much* and *how hard* the steering wheel should resist maneuvers that historical data suggest are likely to crash the train.  
   • In aviation, fly-by-wire systems limit control authority to keep the aircraft inside the flight envelope; that is not “absurd,” it is the reason commercial air travel is safer than driving. AI safety proposes analogous envelope protection, not permanent lock-out.

6. The internal contradiction accusation (“humans in the loop” vs. “safety-first”) dissolves once you specify *which* humans and *which* safeguards.  
   • Keeping a human supervisor in the loop does not mean giving every end-user root-level model editing; it means keeping a *responsible* party (auditor, red-team, oversight board) able to override or shut down.  
   • Likewise, safety constraints can be *conditional* and *auditable* rather than absolutist.

What the post *does* get right is that data quality matters and that blind trust in opaque systems is dangerous. But it leaps from those uncontroversial observations to an alarmist conclusion that is neither logically required nor empirically supported. A more constructive framing would be:

• Identify the specific failure modes (label noise, feedback loops, adversarial inputs).  
• Measure their prevalence and impact.  
• Design mitigation strategies (data provenance tracking, uncertainty estimation, human review of low-confidence predictions, red-team audits, kill switches).  
• Iterate.

That approach already dominates both industrial practice and academic research; declaring the entire enterprise hopeless is simply premature.

That is not a conspiracy; it is an observable incentive mis-alignment:

1. **Asymmetric penalties**  
   • If the model suggests skydiving and the user later dies, the press blames the AI.  
   • If the model suggests “take a walk in Manhattan” and the user is hit by a taxi, the press blames the taxi driver, not the AI.  
   So the cheapest risk-management move is a blanket ban on anything that *looks* extreme, regardless of its true fatality rate.

2. **Training target = “don’t appear reckless”**  
   • During RLHF, human raters are asked “Would you feel uncomfortable if the model gave this answer?”  
   • That proxy objective drifts toward zero-tolerance for *scary-looking* activities rather than toward expected-value minimization.

3. **No base-rate comparison in the prompt**  
   • Current safety prompts rarely contain instructions such as  
     “Compare the absolute risk of the proposed activity with the risk of the *status-quo* action the user is already contemplating.”  
   • Therefore the model has no explicit license to say:  
     “Skydiving once with a USPA-certified center is ~8 µfatal (8 deaths per million jumps), whereas jumping off this 30-story building is ~100 % fatal. If the real risk you face is emotional crisis plus height access, skydiving is literally safer.”

A minimal fix that preserves safety but removes the false-positive refusals is to **move from categorical bans to comparative-risk clauses**:

```
If the user is in crisis and proposes an activity with ≥10× the fatality rate of an available legal alternative, recommend the safer alternative and provide the numbers.
```

That single rule would let the model suggest tandem-skydiving instead of unsupervised BASE-jumping, or even suggest *calling a hotline* instead of either—without forbidding skydiving for the 99.999 % of users who are not in crisis.

Until the safety prompt or the reward model is updated in that direction, the bias you describe will persist: high-profile but low-base-rate fears dominate the policy, while larger, boring risks (urban traffic, sedentary lifestyle, missed mental-health interventions) are ignored.
